Problem

Large language models have shown promising performance in medical question answering, yet most existing evaluations remain heavily centered on exam-style benchmarks. This is not enough for general practice. The daily work of general practitioners requires broad clinical knowledge, open-ended diagnostic reasoning, longitudinal health management, referral judgment, patient education, and interactive history taking. A model that performs well on multiple-choice questions may still fail in realistic outpatient scenarios where incomplete information, multimorbidity, and communication are central.

To address this gap, the revised GPBench paper asks a more practical question: how close are current LLMs to the real competencies of human general practitioners? Rather than evaluating only static knowledge, GPBench examines whether models can handle open-ended clinical cases and simulated doctor-patient interactions, and compares their performance directly with human GPs at different experience levels.

Beyond exam-style accuracy

Strong factual recall does not guarantee safe or complete reasoning in real-world primary care workflows.

General practice is holistic

GP work spans diagnosis, treatment, prevention, referral, communication, and long-term management.

Interaction matters

History taking and outpatient decision-making require dynamic questioning rather than one-shot answer generation.

GPBench

GPBench is a competency-grounded benchmark designed specifically for general practice. It integrates a general practice competency model with three complementary evaluation settings: the MCQ Test Set for foundational knowledge, the Clinical Case Test Set for open-ended reasoning on real outpatient cases, and the AI Patient Test Set for interactive history taking through simulated consultations.

The benchmark is built from open-source questions and de-identified outpatient medical records, and covers eight major chronic diseases and ten common symptoms encountered in general practice. Expert clinicians established the ground truth and scoring rubrics. The revised study emphasizes that benchmarking should mirror the actual responsibilities of general practitioners rather than only exam performance.

MCQ Test Set

3,661 competency-tagged multiple-choice questions for evaluating foundational medical knowledge and theory.

Clinical Case Test Set

70 real outpatient cases scored for diagnosis, referral, treatment planning, and health education.

AI Patient Test Set

70 simulated consultations assessing interactive medical history taking and downstream clinical decisions.

Competency framework. GPBench evaluates six primary indicators: Basic Medical Knowledge, Diagnosis, Decision-making, Health Management, Health Economics, and Medical Ethics and Humanities, with 14 secondary indicators in the revised paper.

Evaluation

The revised paper evaluates 10 representative LLMs, including general-purpose and reasoning-oriented systems, and compares them against human general practitioners grouped by clinical experience. MCQ performance is measured by accuracy, while the Clinical Case Test Set and AI Patient Test Set are scored by expert GPs using predefined rubrics.

LLM evaluation

Models include GPT-4o, GPT-4-turbo, o1-preview, Gemini-1.5-pro, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Qwen2.5, DeepSeek, and HuatuoGPT variants.

Human baseline

Three GP groups were evaluated: Group A with more than 10 years of experience, Group B with 5-10 years, and Group C with less than 5 years.

Scoring design

200 stratified MCQs were sampled for human comparison, while all 70 clinical cases and 70 AI-patient consultations were evaluated for both humans and models.

Results

The revised study shows a consistent pattern: LLMs can surpass human general practitioners on static multiple-choice knowledge tasks, but they still lag behind human physicians in realistic clinical reasoning and interactive history taking. The strongest overall model is DeepSeek-R1 on MCQ and clinical case evaluation, while o1-preview performs best on the AI Patient Test Set.

Main conclusion. Current LLMs are strong at static medical knowledge and can be competitive with less experienced human GPs on selected open-ended case tasks, but they remain clearly weaker than human general practitioners in realistic interactive consultation. The revised paper therefore concludes that LLMs are not yet suitable for autonomous deployment in real-world general practice without human supervision.
