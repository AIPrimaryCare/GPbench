General Practice Benchmark

Evaluating clinical competencies of large language models as general practitioners.

GPBench is a competency-grounded benchmark designed to test whether large language models can support the real work of general practice, from knowledge recall and diagnosis to treatment planning, health education, and patient interaction.

Problem

Why general practice needs a different benchmark.

Traditional medical LLM benchmarks focus heavily on exam-style question answering. That measures factual recall, but it does not capture the broader competence required in general practice, where diagnosis, treatment decisions, multimorbidity, communication, and history taking must work together.

Static knowledge is not enough

Strong MCQ results can hide weak open-ended reasoning when models must explain, prioritize, and manage uncertainty.

General practice is holistic

GP work is symptom-oriented, cross-system, and strongly shaped by referral judgment, prevention, and patient education.

Interaction changes the task

History taking depends on asking the right follow-up questions, not only returning one polished final answer.

GPBench

A competency model paired with three complementary test sets.

GPBench combines a Delphi-designed competency framework with multiple evaluation formats built from open-source questions, real outpatient records, and simulated AI patients.

Evaluation

How the benchmark scores models and human physicians.

MCQs are scored by accuracy, while clinical cases and AI-patient conversations are scored by expert general practitioners using predefined rubrics. The paper also compares LLMs with three groups of human GPs.

Three evaluation modes

  • MCQ Test Set: automatic accuracy over competency-tagged questions.
  • Clinical Case Test Set: expert scoring of diagnosis, referral, treatment, and education.
  • AI Patient Test Set: expert scoring of dialogue-based medical history taking.

Human reference groups

  • Group A: 2 GPs with more than 10 years of experience.
  • Group B: 2 GPs with 5-10 years of experience.
  • Group C: 2 GPs with less than 5 years of experience.

Human MCQ evaluation used a 200-question stratified sample. All 70 clinical cases and all 70 AI-patient dialogues were scored for both humans and LLMs.

Results

10 representative LLMs were benchmarked against human general practitioners.

The overall pattern is clear: LLMs can outperform human physicians on static knowledge tasks, but they still lag behind in realistic case reasoning and interactive patient interviewing.

Main findings

LLMs are strongest on static medical knowledge and comparatively better at health education, but they still show clear weaknesses in diagnostic chain construction, treatment decision-making, and interactive information gathering. The paper concludes that current models are not ready for autonomous deployment in general practice and still require human supervision.
